Article 14: Equality before law

Understanding the foundation of the Rule of Law in India.

The State shall not deny to any person equality before the law or the equal protection of the laws within the territory of India.

In Simple English

Article 14 is the bedrock of democracy in India. It essentially says two things, which sound similar but have slightly different meanings:

1. Equality before the law (A British concept): This means no one is above the law. Whether you are a billionaire, a politician, or an ordinary citizen, the law applies to everyone equally. It is a "negative" concept because it prohibits the State from giving special privileges to anyone.

2. Equal protection of the laws (An American concept): This is a "positive" concept. It means that people in similar situations should be treated similarly. The law shouldn't treat a rich person and a poor person identically if treating them identically would result in unfairness (e.g., taxing a billionaire and a daily wage worker at the exact same flat rate).

Therefore, Article 14 allows for reasonable classification. The government can group people and make different laws for different groups, provided the grouping makes logical sense and is related to the goal the law is trying to achieve.

Crucial Exceptions

  • The President and Governors (Article 361): They enjoy immunity from criminal proceedings during their term of office. They cannot be arrested or imprisoned while in office.
  • Foreign Sovereigns and Diplomats: They enjoy immunity from civil and criminal jurisdiction in India.
  • Members of Parliament (Article 105): No MP is liable to proceedings in a court for anything said or any vote given by them in Parliament.

Landmark Cases

State of West Bengal v. Anwar Ali Sarkar (1952)

The Supreme Court established the twin test for reasonable classification: (1) It must be founded on an intelligible differentia, and (2) The differentia must have a rational nexus to the object sought to be achieved.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

The Court held that Article 14 strikes at arbitrariness in State action and ensures fairness and equality of treatment. Equality and arbitrariness are sworn enemies.
