Article 32: Constitutional Remedies

The Heart and Soul of the Constitution.

(1) The right to move the Supreme Court by appropriate proceedings for the enforcement of the rights conferred by this Part is guaranteed. (2) The Supreme Court shall have power to issue directions or orders or writs, including writs in the nature of habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, quo warranto and certiorari, whichever may be appropriate, for the enforcement of any of the rights conferred by this Part.

In Simple English

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar famously called Article 32 the "heart and soul" of the Constitution. Why? Because without it, all the other fundamental rights (like freedom of speech, equality, life) would be meaningless.

A right is only useful if there is a way to enforce it when someone violates it. Article 32 is that enforcement mechanism. It gives every citizen the fundamental right to approach the Supreme Court of India directly if their fundamental rights are violated by the State.

The Supreme Court can then issue "writs" (formal legal orders) to the government to stop the violation or correct the wrong. The five main types of writs are:

  • Habeas Corpus: "To have the body of." Used to release a person who has been detained unlawfully.
  • Mandamus: "We command." Issued to a public official asking them to perform their official duties.
  • Prohibition: Issued by a higher court to a lower court to prevent it from exceeding its jurisdiction.
  • Certiorari: "To be certified." Issued by a higher court to a lower court to transfer a pending case or squash an order.
  • Quo Warranto: "By what authority." Issued to inquire into the legality of a claim which a person asserts to a public office.

Crucial Exceptions

  • Only for Fundamental Rights: You cannot use Article 32 to enforce legal or statutory rights; it is strictly limited to the Fundamental Rights listed in Part III of the Constitution.
  • National Emergency: Under Article 359, the President can suspend the right to move any court for the enforcement of fundamental rights during a National Emergency (except Articles 20 and 21).
  • Armed Forces: Under Article 33, Parliament can restrict the fundamental rights of the armed forces to ensure discipline.

Landmark Cases

Bandhua Mukti Morcha v. Union of India (1984)

The Supreme Court expanded the scope of Article 32 through Public Interest Litigation (PIL), stating that in cases involving the poor or marginalized who cannot afford legal representation, the court can treat a simple letter from a public-spirited individual as a writ petition.
