Article 21: Protection of life and personal liberty

The heart of the Fundamental Rights.

No person shall be deprived of his life or personal liberty except according to procedure established by law.

In Simple English

Article 21 is often considered the most important fundamental right in the Constitution. It guarantees that the State cannot arbitrarily take away your life or your freedom.

Over the decades, the Supreme Court has dramatically expanded the definition of "life." It no longer just means the right to breathe and survive. It means the right to live with human dignity. Because of this broad interpretation, Article 21 now includes the right to a clean environment, the right to privacy, the right to free legal aid, the right to a speedy trial, and even the right to sleep.

The phrase "procedure established by law" originally meant that as long as a law existed to take away your liberty, it was valid. However, since the landmark Maneka Gandhi case (1978), the law itself must be "just, fair, and reasonable." This essentially brought the American concept of "due process of law" into the Indian Constitution.

Crucial Exceptions

  • Procedure Established by Law: The State can deprive a person of life (e.g., the death penalty) or liberty (e.g., imprisonment) if it strictly follows a valid, fair, and reasonable legal procedure.
  • Preventive Detention: Under Article 22, the State can detain a person without trial under specific national security or public order laws, which acts as a major limitation on Article 21.

Landmark Cases

A.K. Gopalan v. State of Madras (1950)

Initially, the Supreme Court took a narrow view, holding that "procedure established by law" simply meant that any enacted law was sufficient, regardless of whether it was fair or unjust.

Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)

The Court completely reversed the A.K. Gopalan decision, ruling that the procedure prescribing the deprivation of life or liberty must be "right, just and fair," effectively importing the "due process" clause into Article 21.

K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India (2017)

A historic nine-judge bench unanimously ruled that the Right to Privacy is an intrinsic part of the right to life and personal liberty under Article 21.
