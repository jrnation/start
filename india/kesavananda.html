Kesavananda Bharati v. State of Kerala (1973)

The case that saved Indian democracy.

"Parliament's power to amend the Constitution under Article 368 is vast but not absolute. It does not include the power to alter or destroy the 'Basic Structure' or framework of the Constitution." - The Basic Structure Doctrine.

In Simple English

Imagine the Constitution as a house. The Parliament has the power to repaint the walls, change the furniture, and maybe even add a new room (by amending the Constitution). But does the Parliament have the power to destroy the foundation of the house so that it collapses?

That was the central question in the Kesavananda Bharati case. The government argued that under Article 368, Parliament had unlimited power to amend any part of the Constitution, including taking away fundamental rights.

In a historic 7-6 decision, a 13-judge bench of the Supreme Court (the largest bench ever) ruled that while Parliament can amend any part of the Constitution, it cannot destroy its "Basic Structure."

What is the Basic Structure? The Court didn't provide a final list, but it includes things like Democracy, Secularism, the Rule of Law, Judicial Review, and the Separation of Powers. If Parliament passes an amendment that damages these core principles, the Supreme Court will strike it down as unconstitutional.

Impact and Significance

  • Saved Democracy: It prevented the Parliament from turning India into a totalitarian state by rewriting the Constitution entirely.
  • Judicial Supremacy in Interpretation: It established the Supreme Court as the ultimate guardian and interpreter of the Constitution.
  • Enduring Legacy: The "Basic Structure Doctrine" has been used to strike down several unconstitutional amendments since 1973, most notably during the 1975 Emergency.
